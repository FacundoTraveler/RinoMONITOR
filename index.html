Learn about oral breathing

Based on 24 peer-reviewed scientific references

⚠️ Why this matters

Mouth breathing in children leads to serious developmental issues affecting their entire life. Affects an estimated 70% of children in Latin America. [2,3]

Immediate effects
• Dry mouth and bad breath
• Snoring & sleep disruption [12,19]
• Frequent respiratory infections
• Poor concentration at school [3]
Long-term consequences
• Facial growth abnormalities [1,17]
• Dental problems & crowding [6,15]
• Speech development issues
• ADHD-like behavior [3]
🧠
Brain development

Nasal breathing optimizes O₂ delivery to the developing brain, improving focus and memory formation. [3]

😊
Facial growth

Proper tongue posture guides normal jaw and skeletal development during critical growth periods. [17,20]

😴
Sleep quality

Mouth breathing is directly linked to SAOS and sleep-disordered breathing in children. [1,4,19]

🦷
Orthodontic risk

Pediatricians and orthodontists must coordinate early referral to prevent malocclusion. [22,23]

Adenoid facies — the vicious cycle [18]

Early monitoring with RinoMONITOR breaks this cycle.

When to consult an ENT / Orthodontist
Red flags [23]
• Mouth breathing >2 weeks
• Snoring or sleep disturbances
• Open mouth at rest
• Adenoid / tonsil enlargement
Treatment options [10,22]
• Adenoid/tonsil evaluation
• Allergy testing & treatment
• Myofunctional therapy
• Orthodontic referral
✅ Proper breathing technique
Correct posture
✓ Mouth closed at all times
✓ Tongue resting on roof of mouth
✓ Lips gently together
✓ Breathe only through nose
✓ Shoulders relaxed, head straight
Daily exercises
• "Tongue to roof" for 2 minutes
• Blow bubbles to strengthen lips
• Nasal breathing during activities
• Monitor posture every hour
• Practice swallowing correctly
Physiological basis [11,13]

Nasal breathing filters, humidifies and warms air, produces nitric oxide (vasodilator), and activates lower lung lobes. Harvold's landmark primate experiments (1972) demonstrated that forced oral breathing produces measurable craniofacial changes.

Breastfeeding & prevention [7,14]

Breastfeeding promotes proper nasal breathing patterns and reduces risk of dentofacial anomalies. Studies confirm its protective role in naso-oral function development from infancy.

Oral function reduction [16]

Masutomi et al. (2024) found mouth breathing in adolescents significantly reduces tongue pressure and oral motor function — measurable outcomes RinoMONITOR helps prevent through continuous monitoring.
